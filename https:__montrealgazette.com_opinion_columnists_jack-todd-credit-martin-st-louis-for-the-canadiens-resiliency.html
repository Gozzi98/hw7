St. Louis is a rare creature in the world of pro sports, the superstar player who is well on his way to becoming an excellent coach.

It cant possibly go on. The Canadiens have 11 points in eight games, which puts them on track for something north of 110 points on the season. It wont happen. Not with Kirby Dach out for the season, not with forwards Josh Anderson and Juraj Slafkovsky with a single assist apiece after eight games, not with the striped shirts raining penalties every time the Habs take the ice.

Still, its fun while it lasts. This is a remarkably resilient team, especially for one loaded with young players. Most of the credit has to go to head coach Martin St. Louis.

Remember the heady spring of 1971, when Al MacNeil won a Stanley Cup and was all but ridden out of town on a rail for his troubles?

Even hockeys ultimate prize guarantees nothing with Montreals demanding fans but if you took a poll today, Im almost certain youd find that St. Louis was the most popular Habs coach in the long and glorious history of the bleu-blanc-rouge. Dick Irvin Sr., Toe Blake and Scotty Bowman were respected but never loved, in part because they werent francophone.

St. Louis is loved by the players, by the media, by the fans. He is that rarest creature in the world of professional sports, the superstar player who is well on his way to becoming an excellent coach, a feat that eluded all-time greats from Ted Williams to Wayne Gretzky.

Its hard to imagine anyone else who could convince a team that they can come back from a two-goal deficit, kill penalties that spilled into overtime and still win two games in a row.

St. Louis is even funny. When someone asked after the home opener whether it might be good that his club was getting so many chances to practice the penalty kill, St. Louis said with a rueful grin, Im OK with working on it in practice.

***

What does it take? I was at the Bell Centre for two of the more tense moments in Canadiens history the horrific injuries to forwards Trent McCleary and Donald Audette.

On Jan. 29, 2000, McCleary hurled himself in front of a shot that shattered his larynx and came within seconds of dying so close that had the injury happened at the far end of the ice from medical attention, he might not have made it.

On Dec. 1, 2001, it was Donald Audette who almost lost his arm and his life after having the tendons in his arm severed by a skate blade. The medical effort was again heroic, and Audette made it back for the playoffs.

Former Pittsburgh Penguins player Adam Johnson wasnt so fortunate. He lost his life this weekend after a skate blade cut his throat.

The hockey world was left in shock after Johnsons death. It shouldnt have taken this but there have been calls from all sides to make neck guards mandatory at every level of hockey, the NHL included.

NHL players Clint Malarchuk and Richard Zednik both survived after having their throats cut but they were fortunate two years ago, nothing changed after 16-year-old Teddy Balkind died from a similar injury while playing for a high-school team in Connecticut.

This time, you would hope, the players wont be intractable. This was a frightening, tragic incident one that got everyones attention. It calls for more than thoughts and prayers.

Everythings Jake: Jake Allen has to be the early season MVP for the Canadiens, with nods to both Mike Matheson and Sean Monahan. He has stolen each of the three games he has won, which is more than you can ask of a goalie who started the season as goaltender 1B in Montreals three-man rotation.

But Allen is also 33 years old on a club where the average age is Hasnt Started to Shave, so he doesnt figure in the long-term plans.

Globals Brian Wilde has come up with a brilliant trade suggestion, which means it wont happen because it makes too much sense: deal Allen to the improved but goalie-starved Buffalo Sabres. Brian didnt point this out, so I will: Allen could team up with Buffalos biggest sports superstar for a memorable ad campaign: Follow the Allen Brothers, Jake and Josh, to Crustys Cadillac today!
